Cost of Attendance: The Full Picture

Tuition is usually the first number families see, but it's only one piece of the puzzle. The full Cost of Attendance (COA) includes:

  • Tuition and fees
  • Housing
  • Meals
  • Books and supplies
  • Transportation
  • Personal expenses

That's everything it takes to get through one year.

Depending on the school, non-tuition costs — housing, meals, and everyday living expenses — can account for 60–65% of the total bill. This is especially true at in-state public universities where tuition is relatively low.

Living costs are fairly similar no matter where your student attends. Whether it's a state school or a private university, expect to pay roughly $20,000–$24,000 per year for housing and meals. The real pricing differences between schools come down to tuition.

Today's Pricing Tiers

College prices vary widely depending on the type of school. Here's a general sense of where things stand, so your family has a frame of reference as you explore options.

Private universities typically run $85,000–$100,000 per year. Schools like USC and NYU are at or approaching $100,000. That sounds steep — but private schools often have the most financial aid available, which can bring the actual cost down significantly.

In-state public universities range from $25,000–$40,000 per year. If your student attends a state flagship in your home state, this is usually the lowest sticker price available.

Out-of-state public universities fall between $50,000–$80,000 per year. Without the in-state tuition benefit, families pay a premium — and most out-of-state publics offer little to no need-based aid to non-residents.

Sticker Price vs. Net Price

This is one of the most important things to understand early: most students do not pay the full sticker price. The majority receive some combination of need-based grants, merit scholarships, or both.

Net price is what your family actually pays after that free money — grants and scholarships — is subtracted. This is the number that matters when comparing schools.

Sticker Price (Full COA) − Grants & Scholarships = Net Price

Here's how this plays out in practice. A private university with a $99,000 sticker price might award $50,000 in grants and scholarships. Your family's net price: $49,000. An in-state public at $35,000 with no aid costs exactly $35,000. In this scenario, the "expensive" school actually costs more — but for some families, the private school could be the better deal.
