[T4] How Merit Aid Is Awarded

How colleges award merit scholarships — and what your family needs to know about each type.

YOUR KEY TAKEAWAYS

  • Merit is an enrollment tool. When it comes to awarding scholarships, colleges fall into three categories: a few that don’t offer merit at all, those that offer it selectively to top admits, and those that use it broadly to fill their class.
  • There are three main ways colleges award merit: admissions scholarships (automatic), named scholarships or scholars programs (separate application), and department or donor-funded scholarships (student matches and applies).
  • Private scholarships can help, but the math strongly favors institutional merit. Only 7% of undergraduates receive outside scholarships, and the average award is about $2,500 — typically a one-time award, not renewable.

College Merit Policies

Merit is used by Admissions as a recruitment and enrollment tool, but not every school needs to use it to build their class. Colleges generally fall into three categories:

No merit scholarships

Extremely high-demand schools don’t need to use merit to enroll their admitted students — the Ivies and peers like Georgetown and Stanford. Without qualifying for need-based aid, families should expect to pay the full cost of attendance at these institutions.

Exclusive merit to top admits

These schools use merit to recruit their highest-performing applicants through named scholarships or scholar programs. Fewer than 25% of students who do not qualify for need-based aid receive a merit scholarship. At some schools in this tier, more than 90% of students without need-based aid pay full price.

Incentive merit

A majority of colleges today use merit to actively recruit admits. At these schools, anywhere from 25% to nearly 100% of students receive some form of tuition discount.

The Different Ways Colleges Award Merit

Many schools use more than one approach. Here’s how we’d break down the three dominant types.

Admissions scholarships

Also referred to as tuition discounting, these are partial tuition awards, renewable for four years. Students are automatically considered through the admissions application. Some schools require earlier submission of the admissions application for merit consideration.

Named scholarships or scholars programs

These scholarships are often full tuition or full cost of attendance. Given the amount and prestige, these scholarships require a separate application that can involve essays, supplementals, or school nominations. Deadlines are earlier than the deadlines for regular admissions.

Department or donor-funded scholarships

These scholarships are offered by specific donors, schools, or departments within a university. The amounts vary and can sometimes be one-time awards. Students match and apply through the admissions portal’s scholarship database.

Understanding Private Scholarships

Many families hope to build affordability through private scholarships. The data on private scholarships gives some important context: only 7% of undergraduates receive outside scholarships, and the average award is about $2,500. Unlike institutional merit, these are typically one-time awards.

If you do pursue outside scholarships, use free databases like Bold.org, College Board’s Big Future, and Fastweb. But there’s an important caveat: if your student receives need-based aid, colleges may reduce their own aid dollar-for-dollar when an outside scholarship is reported. Most will offset loans and work-study first, but it can ultimately impact grants as well.

The math favors institutional aid. A $20,000 renewable merit scholarship is worth $80,000 over four years.

COMMON QUESTIONS

Does applying Early Decision affect merit scholarship offers?

It can. Colleges are equally committed to providing need-based aid regardless of when a student applies. But merit scholarships are an enrollment incentive — a school may have less motivation to offer merit to an Early Decision applicant who’s already committed. For families where merit is an important part of affordability, an Early Action or Regular Decision strategy lets you compare offers across schools before committing.

Are there schools that publish what merit award my student might receive based on GPA or test scores?

Yes — many four-year public universities and some private colleges chart their admissions scholarships by GPA and test score ranges. This can give your family a useful sense of potential merit before your student even applies. Check the admissions website of schools on your list to see if they publish a merit chart.

How realistic is it to fund college through outside (private) scholarships?

It shouldn’t be your primary strategy. Only about 7% of undergraduates receive outside scholarships, and the average award is roughly $2,500 — typically a one-time award, not renewable. A $20,000 renewable institutional merit scholarship is worth $80,000 over four years. If you do pursue outside scholarships, use free databases like Bold.org, College Board’s Big Future, and Fastweb.
