[T3] How Need-Based Aid Is Awarded

How colleges use your financial information to calculate your eligibility for need-based grants and aid.

Video: Watch the overview

6 min
7 min read

YOUR KEY TAKEAWAYS

  • How much need-based aid your student is eligible for depends on two things: your family’s calculated ability to pay (the Student Aid Index) and each college’s cost of attendance. Because both vary, eligibility is different at every school.
  • Eligibility alone doesn’t determine the award. Each college has its own policies and resources that determine how much of your student’s need they’ll actually meet — and many schools can’t cover 100%.
  • Your net price will vary widely from school to school. The most expensive school based on sticker price isn’t always the most expensive after aid. Calculate each college’s net price before crossing any school off your list.

Your Student’s Calculated Eligibility

Need-based aid is awarded on two key factors. The first is your student’s calculated aid eligibility at a particular school.

Colleges calculate your ability to pay using one of two applications:

  1. the FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid), used by most colleges
  2. the CSS Profile, required by roughly 160 mostly private colleges.

Both applications calculate your contribution for one year, which is called your Student Aid Index (SAI).


Every financial aid office then uses this equation:

Cost of Attendance

(Sticker Price)

–  Student Aid Index

(Your Contribution)

=  Demonstrated Need

(Your Eligibility)

The same family with the same financial profile will have different eligibility at schools because the cost of attendance and calculated contribution both vary.

Schools’ Policies to Award Need-Based Grants

The second factor for need-based awards: what amount of your student’s need or eligibility will a school actually award? Each school reports a key data point each year — the average percentage of need they meet.

  • Some private colleges meet 100% of need. Schools like the Ivies and other highly-resourced institutions commit to fully funding every admitted student’s eligibility.
  • Many colleges meet less than 100%. A school at 71% leaves a ga, oreligibility that goes unmet and becomes part of your out-of-pocket cost.
  • Out-of-state publics generally do not provide need-based grants to non-residents. Their funding is targeted to resident students. Only the Pell Grant is available.

But, because they meet 100% of need, Northeastern is actually the second most affordable after aid. Insights from Net Price Calculators

Net price calculators are available on every college’s financial aid website. You input financials anonymously to get an estimated aid package and net price (your price or investment after deducting need-based grants or merit scholarships).

There’s also MyinTuition, a quick estimator covering about 65 schools that asks fewer questions. It’s a good starting point, but the school-specific net price calculator will give you more detailed resultsand can help with scenario building as your finances change.

The number of questions a calculator asks indicates the quality of the estimate. CSS Profile school calculators tend to produce higher-fidelity results.

Your net price will vary widely school to school. Many families feel ineligible after running one calculator, then find significant eligibility at other schools.

COMMON QUESTIONS

What's the difference between the FAFSA and the CSS Profile?

Both calculate your ability to pay, but they use different methodologies. The FAFSA is required for federal and state aid and used by most colleges. The CSS Profile is required by about 160 mostly private colleges and collects more detailed information — including home equity and, for divorced families, both households. Your Student Aid Index can differ between the two, which is why eligibility varies from school to school.

Do colleges consider siblings in college when calculating aid?

It depends on the application. The FAFSA no longer factors in a sibling attending college at the same time — that used to lower your contribution, but it's no longer the case. CSS Profile colleges, however, do preserve what's known as the sibling discount. Some private colleges will also consider expenses for younger siblings in K–12 private education if you advocate for it.

How do we know if we can trust a net price calculator estimate?

As a rule of thumb, the more questions a calculator asks, the more reliable the estimate. CSS Profile school calculators tend to produce higher-fidelity results because they collect more detailed financial information. If a calculator only asks about income ranges without asset details, treat it as a rough guide. Also keep in mind that calculators for divorced or separated families can be limited — they're built for a single household.
