YOUR KEY TAKEAWAYS

  • Financial aid falls into two categories: gift aid (grants and scholarships you don’t repay) and self-help aid (student loans and work-study). Gift aid actually reduces what your family pays.
  • Federal Aid options include the Pell Grant, Direct Federal Student Loans and work-study. These are the smallest portion of financial aid at private institutions.
  • For institutional aid, there are two distinct paths to financial assistance - need-based grants (awarded by financial aid based on your finances) and merit-based scholarships (awarded by admissions based on your student’s performance). You can take advantage of both.

Types of Financial Assistance

Need-based grants can come from three sources. The federal government has the Pell Grant program. Your state may have need-based grant funding, though students need to stay in-state to use it. And colleges themselves offer institutional grants. At private institutions in particular, this is the source for the majority of funding for need-qualified students.

There are also merit-based scholarships, increasingly common at both private and public institutions. These are independent of the need evaluation and based on a student’s standing in admissions.

Finally, there’s self-help aid — work-study and student loans offered through the federal government. It’s called self-help because it asks students to work or borrow to pay for expenses.

A Deeper Dive into Federal Aid Options

The Pell Grant is targeted at families with the highest need — typically parent income in the $70,000 range or lower, though family size matters.

Work-study is more common. Students find campus opportunities like research and office work to earn money toward college expenses.

Direct Student Loans are the only undergraduate loan program that doesn’t require an adult co-signer. Subsidized loans don’t accrue interest while in school; unsubsidized loans do. Any FAFSA filer, regardless of qualifying for need-based aid, can access the unsubsidized loan.

Undergraduate direct loans are very small — they’re not a way to finance an education. First-year borrowing is capped at $5,500, second year at $6,500, and the final two years at $7,500 each.

Two Paths to Financial Assistance

For institutional aid, there are two paths: need-based grants and merit-based scholarships. These are distinctive, primarily in how the money is allocated.

A student who isn’t need-qualified at a certain institution can still find a path to affordability through merit. Need-based grant awards depend not just on eligibility, but on a school’s resources. Colleges are differently resourced and may be more or less generous.

As we go through future topics, we’re going to talk a lot about net price — your true investment after subtracting grants and scholarships from the sticker price. Most students receive some form of assistance, so by focusing on sticker price alone, we might not be focusing on the right number.

When colleges build an offer, they use

  1. federal and state aid first,
  2. then factor in merit scholarships from admissions,
  3. finally add institutional need-based grants when a student’s need exceeds what is met with federal and state aid and merit scholarships.

This can be surprising — families often think of merit as a bonus, but colleges treat it as a building block toward meeting the student’s need.

COMMON QUESTIONS

If we're pretty sure we won't get need-based aid, should we still file the FAFSA?

It's worth it. Any student who completes the FAFSA — even if they're not need-qualified — can access the federal unsubsidized loan, which doesn't require a co-signer. Many state grant programs also require a FAFSA on file. And some colleges use FAFSA data as part of their merit scholarship process. Filing keeps your options open.

Can my student receive both need-based aid and merit scholarships?

Yes — and many students do. When a college builds a financial aid offer, merit scholarships from admissions are factored in alongside need-based grants from the financial aid office. They work together to reduce your net price. A student doesn't have to choose one path or the other.

Does applying for financial aid affect my student's chances of admission?

For need-based aid, most colleges evaluate admission and financial aid separately. Your student won't be penalized for applying. For merit scholarships, those are actually part of the admissions process — they're used to recruit students the school wants to enroll. Applying for aid and being considered for merit are both normal parts of the process.
